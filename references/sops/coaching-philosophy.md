# Coaching Philosophy — The Big 3 Overlay

## Core Principle

It's not **what** you do, it's **how** you do it and **what your intention is**.

The Big 3 Checklist is not a separate section of exercises. It is a **movement quality framework** that gets applied to every exercise in the program. Every rep of every exercise should be performed through this lens.

## The Big 3 Checklist

These three checkpoints are cued at the start of every session (with dedicated priming exercises) AND embedded into the coaching cues of every exercise where relevant.

### 1. Foot Position / Mechanics
- **The check:** Is my arch engaged? Am I in tripod stance? Where is my weight distributed?
- **Why it matters:** Proper foot position builds the foundation that prevents knee valgus, protects the ACL, and creates a stable base for force production up the entire chain.
- **When to cue:** Every standing exercise, every lunge, every jump, every landing. If feet are on the ground, this applies.

### 2. Rib Cage Alignment / Bracing
- **The check:** Are my ribs flared anteriorly? Is my cage buttoned down toward my belly button? Am I bracing properly through my core?
- **Why it matters:** Anterior rib flare disconnects the core, puts the low back in extension, disengages the diaphragm, and creates compensatory strain up through the shoulders, neck, and brachial plexus. Proper alignment protects the back, trains the core, and maintains good upper body posture.
- **When to cue:** Every exercise. There is no exercise where rib cage position doesn't matter.

### 3. Breathing Mechanics
- **The check:** Am I using the right breathing strategy for this exercise? Am I breathing efficiently while maintaining my brace?
- **Why it matters:** Breathing drives bracing. The wrong breathing pattern undermines core stability, leaks power, and creates compensatory tension. The right pattern enhances performance and protects the body.
- **When to cue:** Every exercise. Different exercises require different breathing strategies (see Breathing Mechanics Types below).

## Breathing Mechanics Types

Taught in this order. Each builds on the last.

### 1. Passive Breathing (`passive`)
- **What:** Pure diaphragmatic breathing. No core brace.
- **Purpose:** Regulate the nervous system down. Learn to pull air with the diaphragm, not the chest.
- **When to use:** Cool-downs, recovery, nervous system regulation, the very first breathing drills in a program. Step one to introducing breathing mechanics.
- **Example exercises:** Crocodile breathing, supine diaphragmatic breathing, box breathing.

### 2. Sub-Maximal Effort Breathing (`sub-max`)
- **What:** Core brace applied at medium effort while maintaining 360-degree expansion breathing. Breathe fully THROUGH the brace -- don't sacrifice oxygen intake.
- **Purpose:** Train the core, protect the back, and feed oxygen efficiently under load. This is the default breathing for nearly all exercises.
- **When to use:** Bird dogs, crawling variations, lunges, squats (moderate load), presses (moderate load), mobility drills, CARS, arm care work -- essentially everything that isn't max effort or explosive.
- **The hack:** Most people can't maintain good breathing under a brace and sacrifice oxygen intake, which kills performance. An athlete who masters sub-max breathing will appear more in shape than someone who doesn't breathe well, because they're actually getting oxygen while training.
- **Key coaching point:** Initially teach bracing alone, then layer in the breathing. Don't try to do both at once until the brace is automatic.

### 3. Max Effort Breathing (`max-effort`)
- **What:** Valsalva-style. Pack in a lot of air quickly behind the braced core, tighten down hard, and exhale strongly through the exertion (coming up out of a squat, lockout of a press, etc.).
- **Purpose:** Maximum intra-abdominal pressure for heavy loads. Spine protection under peak force.
- **When to use:** Heavy squats, deadlifts, heavy presses, max-effort strength work.
- **Key coaching point:** The inhale is fast and aggressive -- pack air behind the brace. The exhale is timed to the hardest part of the lift and is forceful, not passive.

### 4. Dynamic Effort Breathing (`dynamic`)
- **What:** Maintaining a brace (but less intense than max effort) while using the breath to CREATE explosiveness from the core. Short, sharp exhales timed to the movement.
- **Purpose:** Power transfer, fast-twitch activation, staying light and explosive.
- **When to use:** Plyometrics, med ball work, bounding, agility drills, reactive movements. Think of a boxer's breathing as he hits a bag.
- **Key coaching point:** The exhale drives the explosion. The breath IS the power source, not a side effect of it.

### Nasal vs. Mouth Breathing Rules

- **Default to nasal breathing as much as possible**, especially on inhales. Nasal breathing is the best tool for regulating the nervous system.
- **Passive and sub-max breathing:** Nasal in, nasal out. If you can't nasal breathe, you're working too hard for the breathing type.
- **Max effort:** Mouth inhale is acceptable (packing air fast). Mouth exhale on exertion.
- **Dynamic effort:** Usually exhaling through the mouth (sharp, explosive exhales), but not always. Inhales should still be nasal when possible.
- **Mouth taping:** Used in certain training contexts to reinforce nasal breathing habits. Good for mobility flows, jump rope conditioning, lower-intensity steady-state work.

## The Master Cue: Initiate from the Breath Out

**Every movement starts from the inside out.**

The sequence: Breath → Brace → Feet → Movement.

Athletes should learn to initiate movements from the breath-out, from the braced core-out. This intention roots them into the movement all the way down to their feet. An arm care drill isn't just about the arm -- they're initiating and coordinating the movement from their feet, into their core, from the proper breath, and THEN out to the actual movement.

This changes how athletes perceive exercises. Nothing is isolated. Everything is whole-body, breath-first.

## How This Applies to Programming

1. **Session start:** Every session begins with Big 3 priming exercises -- dedicated drills that wake up foot mechanics, rib cage awareness, and breathing patterns. This primes the brain-muscle connection.

2. **Exercise cues:** Every exercise in the library should have Big 3-relevant cues embedded, not as separate instructions but woven into the coaching language. Example for a lunge:
   - *Instead of:* "Step forward, bend both knees, push back up."
   - *Use:* "Tripod foot on the front leg -- feel all three points. Ribs buttoned down, core braced. Inhale on the way down, exhale to drive back up. Knee tracks over second toe because your arch is holding."

3. **Remote coaching safety net:** When Justus trains without Max watching, the Big 3 awareness IS the coach. If he's checking these three things on every rep, he's self-correcting form, protecting his body, and building habits that will carry into every future training environment (college weight room, practice field, etc.).

4. **Progression criteria:** An exercise isn't "mastered" just because the reps and sets are completed. It's mastered when the Big 3 checkpoints are automatic -- when Justus doesn't have to think about his foot position, rib cage, or breathing because they're habit.

## Why This Matters for Justus Specifically

- **ACL history:** Foot position awareness is his first line of defense against re-injury. Knee valgus comes from the foot, not the knee.
- **Low back pain:** Rib cage alignment and bracing directly address his back issues. Anterior flare = extension stress on the lumbar spine.
- **Quarterback demands:** He'll be in a college strength program soon where coaches may not cue these things. If these are habitual before he arrives, he's protected.
- **Running QB style:** His physical playing style puts extra demand on his body. These fundamentals are what let him absorb contact safely and recover faster.
