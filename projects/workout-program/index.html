The Big 3 Checklist

Three checkpoints I find in every exercise, every drill, every rep:

1. Set My Arches. Build the arch in my foot. Let it spiral my knee and hip into alignment.

2. Set My Brace and Breath. 360 expansion. Nasal breathing. Brace like I'm about to get hit.

3. Set My Scap and Neck. Shoulder blades locked on the rib cage. Neck packed. Eyes level.

These first few weeks, my only job is to find these three checkpoints in everything I do. The exercises are the vehicle -- the checkpoints are the skill.

👣

Foot Position / Mechanics

Short Foot Activation → Balance Drill

3 sets · Build the arch seated, then stand and hold single-leg · Log time per set

Cue: Start with a collapsed arch -- let the foot roll inward. Now rotate out slightly while PULLING THE BALL OF YOUR FOOT BACK TOWARD YOUR HEEL. Feel the arch muscles engage. Build the arch. Once you feel it, stand on that foot. Let the arch engagement gently spiral your knee and femur outward into external rotation. This is the alignment that protects the ACL. Hold as long as you can maintain the arch and the rotation. Progression: Add any other movements while balancing and maintaining arch control -- high knees, RDL, squat, juggle, anything. The arch stays set no matter what.

Heel Elevated Balance (Short Foot + Plantar Flexion ER)

3 sets each foot · Log time per set

Cue: Same setup as Short Foot Activation -- start collapsed, rotate out, pull ball of foot toward heel, build the arch, feel the external rotation spiral up through knee and hip. Once the arch is set, rise onto the balls of your feet and elevate the heels. Now maintain that same slight external rotation at the foot, ankle, knee, and hip while in plantar flexion. The Achilles has to learn to be stable here. Hold as long as the arch and rotation hold. Progressions: Heel elevated squats, iso squats, iso sissy squats, RDL, iso lunge (front leg loading focus), single-leg balance. The arch stays set through all of it.

Bracing / Breathing

360 Breathing

3 sets · 8 breaths per set

Cue: Seated or supine. Hands grabbing your obliques and lower rib cage to feel the breath. Inhale through the nose and expand into ALL directions -- front, sides, and back. Ribs should expand laterally, not rise up. Exhale fully and feel the ribs button down toward the belly button. No chest rise. This is your brace foundation. Brace for a punch around your whole trunk and keep nasal breathing.

Dead Bug (Static → Movement)

4 sets · Set 1: static hold, lock position · Sets 2-4: slow contralateral movement · Log reps per set

Cue: Ribs buttoned down, low back flat on the floor. Set 1 -- just hold the position and breathe through your brace. Nasal breathing only. Own it before you move. Sets 2-4 -- slow opposite arm and leg extension. The moment your back arches off the floor, the set is over. Initiate every rep from the exhale, from the core out.

Bear Crawl (Static Hold → Crawl)

4 sets · Set 1: static hold, knees 1" off ground · Sets 2-4: slow controlled crawling · Log time per set

Cue: Set 1 -- tuck toes, lift knees 1 inch. Hold. Breathe through the brace. If you're shaking, good. Sets 2-4 -- opposite hand and foot move together, slow and controlled. A cup of water on your back should not spill. Synchronize the breath with each step. Nasal breathing only -- if you have to mouth-breathe, slow down.

Full Breathing Technique Progression (Crawl Position)

4 steps · All performed in crawl position · 15 second rest between steps 3 and 4

Step 1 — Passive Breathing: Quadruped, knees on the ground. Pure diaphragmatic nasal breathing. Belly expands on inhale, no chest rise. Feel the breath pull down through the diaphragm. This is your reset baseline.

Step 2 — Sub-Max Effort: Tuck toes, lift knees 1 inch off the ground. Add a medium-effort core brace. Maintain 360 nasal breathing through the brace. Lift opposite hand and foot off the ground, hold for 1-2 full sub-max breaths, then switch sides. The breath should never stop or get shallow -- if it does, you've braced too hard. The goal is nasal breathing THROUGH the brace.

Step 3 — Max Effort: From crawl position (knees up), take one large aggressive nasal inhale. Create a max-effort core brace -- pack as much air behind it as you can. Extend your hands forward and feet back into a more overhead/stretched-out position, placing maximum tension on the core. Hold for 1-2 counts. Return to crawl position (knees up) and resume sub-max nasal breathing. The contrast between max effort and sub-max teaches the difference and builds both capacities.

Step 4 — Dynamic Effort: After a 15-second break and reset, return to crawl position, knees up. Start with sub-max brace and nasal breathing. Then hit 5 quick plyometric push-ups -- very shallow depth, the focus is NOT the push-up but being dynamic with the movement and breath simultaneously. Push little bits of air out like a boxer hitting the bag on each pop. Sharp, short exhales driving each rep. Return to sub-max nasal breathing between rounds.

Set the Scap and Neck

Scapula Push-Ups (Protraction & Retraction)

3 sets · 10 reps · Advance to scap circles

Cue: Push-up position, arms locked out. No elbow bend at any point -- this is pure scapular movement. Let your chest sink between your shoulder blades (full retraction), then push the ground away as hard as you can (full protraction). Slow and controlled. Progression: Scap circles -- combine elevation and depression with protraction and retraction to create circular motions in both directions. This builds the scapular control that protects the shoulder in every throwing position.

Wall Slide with Foam Roller (Serratus Activation)

2 sets · 10-12 reps

Cue: Face the wall, forearms on a foam roller. Slide arms up while pressing into the wall and protracting your shoulder blades. Feel the muscles along the side of your rib cage engage -- that's the serratus anterior. Control the descent for 3 seconds. The serratus is what lets your scapula rotate upward cleanly during a throw. Sub-max nasal breathing throughout.

Wall Slide W-to-Y (Scap Depression + ER)

2 sets · 10 reps

Slide hands up along the wall into an overhead Y position, keeping wrists and elbows in contact the whole way. At the top, emphasize the pull DOWN -- depress the scap while fully retracting and externally rotating the shoulder. The pull-down is the money. Chin packed, eyes level. Nasal breathing throughout.

Mobility

CARS, active end-range strength, and balance integration. Ground up, joint by joint. Move slow, nasal breathe through it, reach your absolute max range.

🦶

Ankle CARS (Supine → Standing Balance)

4 sets each ankle · Sets 1-3: supine, knee pulled to chest · Set 4: standing on opposite leg

Cue: Full circumduction in both directions -- reach your absolute maximum range at every point in the circle. Slow and controlled. Some cramping is normal, that's the edge. Sub-max nasal breathing throughout. On set 4, standing version -- short foot arch on the plant leg, knee/femur externally rotated. The balance IS the progression.

Arm Care

Protect the arm. Rotator cuff health, scapular control, and throwing longevity. Rib cage position drives scapular mechanics -- if the ribs flare, the scapula can't sit on the cage properly, and the shoulder pays for it. Big 3 cues apply here.

🎯

Foundational

Banded Shoulder Dislocations

3 sets · Log reps per set

Cue: Hold a band wide with both hands in front of you. Keeping arms straight, slowly bring the band up and over your head, all the way behind your back, then reverse. The wider your grip, the easier it is -- narrow the grip as mobility improves. Move slow, controlled, max range at every point. This warms up the entire shoulder girdle and teaches the rotator cuff to work through a full arc. Ribs down -- if you flare to get the band overhead, you're compensating with your low back instead of earning the range at the shoulder. Sub-max nasal breathing.

Rehab / Prehab

Targeted work for the knees, hips, and lower back. Build resilience where it matters most. ACL history and low back pain drive every selection here.

🎯

Foundational

Banded Standing VMO Activation

3 sets each leg · Log reps per set

Cue: Mini band just above the knees. Stand on one leg, slight bend in the knee. Push the knee outward against the band (external rotation) while squeezing the VMO at the inside of the knee through terminal extension. The VMO is the last line of defense for patellar tracking and knee stability. Short foot on the stance leg -- the foot drives the knee position. Ribs down, nasal breathe through it.

▶ Watch video

Side-Lying Clamshell (Mini Band)

3 sets each side · 15 reps · 3s eccentric

Cue: Mini band just above the knees. Heels together, knees stacked. Open the top knee against the band without rolling the pelvis backward. Control the lowering for 3 seconds -- the eccentric is where the glute medius gets stronger. Feel it deep in the side of the hip, not the TFL on the front. Why this matters: weak glute medius = femoral internal rotation = knee valgus = the exact mechanism that tore the ACL. This is insurance.

Crawl Position Bird-Dog

3 sets · Log reps per set (alternating sides)

Cue: Bear crawl position -- knees 1 inch off the ground. Extend opposite arm and leg simultaneously without ANY rotation of the pelvis or shoulders. The crawl position makes this significantly harder than the standard quadruped version because your core has to stabilize against gravity while also maintaining the hover. Why this is better than floor bird-dogs: it trains anti-rotation under the same demand you'll face in a pocket, in a scramble, absorbing contact. Sub-max nasal breathing throughout. If your hips shift, the set is over.

Swiss Ball Hamstring Curls

3 sets · Log reps per set

Cue: Lie on your back, heels on a Swiss ball. Lift hips into a bridge, then curl the ball toward your butt by bending the knees. Extend back out with control. Hips stay up the entire time -- if they drop, you've lost the glute-hamstring connection. Why hamstrings matter: they are the ACL's primary dynamic restraint. They resist the exact force that tears the ligament. Strong hamstrings = protected knee. Nasal breathe through the brace, ribs down.

This trains the hamstring at the length where it's most vulnerable to tearing.

3 sets each side · Set 1-2: standard side plank (log time) · Set 3: Copenhagen variation (log time)

Cue: Standard -- elbow under shoulder, feet stacked or staggered. Lift hips, hold a straight line. Squeeze the bottom-side glute. Sissy Squat (Iso → Progressive ROM)

3 sets · Start as isometric holds (log time) · Progress to reps through increasing ROM

Cue: Stand tall, hold a support for balance. Lean back from the knees, keeping a straight line from knees through hips through shoulders -- no hip hinge. Start with small ranges and hold the bottom position isometrically. Why isometric first: the quad tendon and patellar tendon need time to adapt to this loading angle before you ask them to work through full range. As the iso's get comfortable, increase the lean-back depth and add slow reps. This builds quad and VMO strength at the exact knee angles that matter for landing, cutting, and ACL protection. Ribs down, don't arch.

Single-Leg Balance Hops to Stick → Drop Landing Progression

3 sets each leg · Log reps per set · 3s freeze on every landing

Cue: Start with small single-leg hops in place -- hop and stick the landing for 3 full seconds. Knee tracks over second toe. No valgus collapse. Short foot, arch engaged, knee externally rotated from the foot. Once these are clean, progress to forward hops, then lateral hops, then step-off drop landings from a low box. Why we start with balance hops: landing mechanics are what protect the ACL. If you can't stick a small hop without the knee diving in, you're not ready for bigger forces. The stick is the test -- quiet feet, controlled knee, every time. This is the most important exercise in this section for ACL protection.
